Calculate your due date

Choose how you want to date the pregnancy. Most people start with the first day of the last menstrual period (LMP). Your clinician may refine the EDD with an early ultrasound.

Calculation method

Naegele’s rule: EDD = LMP + 280 days, adjusted by (cycle length − 28). Assumes ovulation ~14 days before the next period.

EDD = conception date + 266 days (38 weeks). Best when you tracked ovulation closely or know the conception day.

EDD = scan date + (280 − gestational age in days). ACOG: first-trimester ultrasound is the most accurate dating method when available.

EDD = transfer date + (266 − embryo day). Day 5 transfer → +261 days. IVF dating is often very precise when the transfer day is known.

FAQ

How is my due date calculated from LMP?

We add 280 days to the first day of your last menstrual period, then adjust by (cycle length − 28). This is Naegele’s rule, assuming ovulation about 14 days before the next period. Irregular cycles reduce accuracy — early ultrasound may be more reliable.

Why doesn’t my clinic’s due date match this calculator?

Providers often combine LMP with ultrasound. ACOG recommends revising the EDD when early ultrasound differs meaningfully from LMP dating. Your charted “best obstetric estimate” is what should guide care — treat this tool as educational.

Will I deliver on my exact due date?

Only about 5% of babies arrive on the estimated due date. A full-term window spans roughly 37–42 weeks. ACOG notes pregnancy commonly lasts about 40 weeks ± 2 weeks. Use the EDD for planning, not as a guarantee.

What if my cycle isn’t 28 days?

Enter your average cycle length. Longer cycles usually mean later ovulation, so we add extra days to the EDD; shorter cycles subtract days. Very irregular cycles are better dated with ultrasound when possible.

How does IVF dating work?

With a known transfer date and embryo age, dating is precise: EDD = transfer + (266 − embryo day). A day-5 blastocyst uses +261 days; day-3 uses +263. Follow your fertility clinic’s documented EDD for clinical decisions.

Is this medical advice?

No. This calculator is for education and planning only. Prenatal care, screening schedules and delivery decisions must come from your obstetric provider. Seek urgent care for severe pain, heavy bleeding, or reduced fetal movement as advised by your clinician.
